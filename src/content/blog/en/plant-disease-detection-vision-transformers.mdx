## The Project

FH Wedel Deep Learning course, summer semester 2025. Two people, 12 weeks, 200 combined person-hours. The goal: an automated plant health monitoring system good enough to sit on a real urban farming installation. Given a photo of a leaf, the system should tell you which plant it is, whether it's sick, and if so, what it has.

We built it. It hit 99%+ on isolated leaf images across three plant species and sixteen disease classes. And then the bottom fell out the moment we tested on whole plants. That gap — between the controlled eval set and the real deployment conditions — ended up being the most important thing I learned on this project.

## The Dataset Nobody Wants to Talk About

Before any modelling, there's data quality. We compiled **31,616 images across 3 species (lettuce, tomato, strawberry) and 16 classes** from multiple public sources. The initial look at the raw data was ugly:

- Roughly 30% of the raw dataset was irrelevant — stock photos, prepared salads, non-plant images. Removed via manual review plus embedding-based clustering.
- Duplicate and augmented images were leaking across train, val, and test splits. Left unfixed, every accuracy number would have been lying. We eliminated them with perceptual hashing and cross-split deduplication.
- Labels were inconsistent across sources and needed harmonization.
- The class distribution was heavily skewed — healthy lettuce vastly outnumbered diseased specimens. We addressed this with strategic class merging, inverse-frequency weighting, and focal loss.

The punchline of the data section is the same one every dataset paper ends with and everyone learns the hard way: **data quality beats data quantity.** Targeted curation moved our numbers more than any model swap.

## Hierarchical Classification, Not One Big Model

The obvious first attempt is a single 16-class classifier. The obvious first attempt is also wrong, for two reasons. First, the classes are structurally hierarchical — a tomato disease is not the same kind of thing as a lettuce disease. Second, more than half the real traffic is healthy plants, and routing all of them through a 16-way classifier is wasteful.

We built a three-stage pipeline instead:

1. **PlantID (Stage i)** — ResNet50 routes the image to one of three species. 99.9% accuracy.
2. **Binary Health (Stage ii)** — Per-plant models classify healthy vs. diseased. Healthy samples exit early, which was 55–65% of traffic. Focal loss handled the residual imbalance. 99.7% accuracy.
3. **Disease Classification (Stage iii)** — Per-plant disease models, only trained where a plant had multiple disease classes. Plant-specific routing beat a single global disease classifier by roughly 6 percentage points. 99.1% accuracy.

## The Architecture Comparison

We compared 8+ architectures across three tiers:

- **Transformer tier** — ViT-Base, ViT-Small, DINOv2 (all 86M params)
- **Balanced tier** — ConvNeXt-Base, ConvNeXt-Tiny, ResNet-50
- **Fast tier** — EfficientNet-B0, MobileNetV3 (around 5M params)

In controlled evaluation the top of every tier landed between 99.1% and 99.9%. Strong augmentation (color jitter + perspective transforms) cut the generalization gap by roughly 92% versus no augmentation at all.

The surprising bit: **the fast models were perfectly competitive** on this dataset. EfficientNet-B0 was within a percentage point of ViT-Base at 6% of the parameter count. For a deployed system on an edge device, the choice becomes obvious.

## Explainability: Grad-CAM and Attention Rollout

A model that shouts "diseased" is not useful on a farm. A model that highlights the exact patch of leaf that made it say "diseased" is. We integrated two complementary techniques into the Streamlit dashboard:

- **Grad-CAM** for the CNNs. Gradient-weighted class activation mapping highlights the image regions that pushed the prediction. We auto-detected the correct target layer per architecture so the user didn't have to know.
- **Attention Rollout** for the ViTs. Aggregates attention weights across all transformer layers to trace information flow from patches to the prediction. Shows global context understanding, which matters for diseases distributed across the leaf rather than localized in a spot.

Both overlays got adjustable opacity sliders. Agricultural professionals could toggle and tune until they understood why the model decided what it did. Explainability wasn't a side feature, it was the feature that made domain experts willing to trust the thing at all.

## The Uncomfortable Finding

Week 8. We finally started testing on whole-plant scenes — actual photos of tomato plants in context, with overlapping leaves, variable lighting, soil in the background, different camera angles. The numbers dropped hard.

Models that had scored 99.7% on isolated leaves were degrading significantly outside the lab. That's the **domain gap**, and it's the real bottleneck for agricultural AI deployment — not accuracy on clean benchmarks, not architecture choice, not a slightly better loss function. It's the mismatch between training distribution and deployment distribution.

Our recommendations for future work follow directly from this:

- **Spatial localization first.** A YOLO detector to isolate individual leaves from whole-plant scenes before classification.
- **Training data that looks like deployment.** Whole-plant photos, not only leaf close-ups. Different lighting. Different backgrounds. Different cameras.
- **Test real conditions early.** If we'd run whole-plant trials in week 2 instead of week 8, the entire project would have been shaped differently.

## What I Actually Learned

- **Data quality beats data quantity** — said above, worth saying twice.
- **Hierarchical routing pays off** — +6pp over a global classifier and cleaner downstream models.
- **Transformer vs. CNN is the wrong fight** — for this problem, a 5M-parameter EfficientNet is perfectly competitive with a 86M-parameter ViT.
- **Explainability drives adoption** — Grad-CAM and Attention Rollout did more to build trust with agriculture people than any metric we quoted.
- **Test in real conditions early** — the domain gap was invisible in eval and only surfaced during whole-plant trials. Not accidentally. Systematically.
